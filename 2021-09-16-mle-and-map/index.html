MLE and MAP

Cornell University
September 16, 2021

We will talk about two ways of estimating a parameter: MLE and MAP. Their formulas look really similar to generative learning and discriminative as if one corresponded to generative and the other corresponded to discriminative. However, MLE/MAP has nothing to do with generative/discriminative learning. They just look similar. That’s all.

MLE and Coin Toss¶

Say we have a coin, and we want to find θ=P(H)\theta = P(H)θ=P(H): the probability that this coin comes up heads when we toss it.

We toss it n=10n = 10n=10 times and obtain the following sequence of outcomes: D={H,T,T,H,H,H,T,T,T,T}D=\{H, T, T, H, H, H, T, T, T, T\}D={H,T,T,H,H,H,T,T,T,T}. Therefore, we observed nH=4n_H=4nH​=4 heads and nT=6n_T=6nT​=6 tails. So, intuitively,

P(H)≈nHnH+nT=410=0.4P(H) \approx \frac{n_H}{n_H + n_T} = \frac{4}{10}= 0.4P(H)≈nH​+nT​nH​​=104​=0.4
(1)

. We will derive this more formally with Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)¶

The estimator we just mentioned is the Maximum Likelihood Estimate. In particular, we want to find a distribution that makes the data we observed as likely as possible. MLE is done in two steps:

  1. Make an explicit modeling assumption about what type of distribution your data was sampled from.
  2. Set the parameters of this distribution so that the data you observed is as likely as possible.

Before proceeding to MLE, we already observed our data. Namely, in our coin example, D,n,nH,nTD, n, n_H, n_TD,n,nH​,nT​ are are set values.

We will assume that coin toss is of binomial distribution Bin(n,θ)Bin(n,\theta)Bin(n,θ), where nnn is number of tosses we made and θ\thetaθ is the probability coming up head we are trying to estimate. Formally, given this is a binomial distribution with probability θ\thetaθ, we have

P(D∣θ)=(nH+nTnH)θnH(1−θ)nT,\begin{align} P(D\mid \theta) &= \begin{pmatrix} n_H + n_T \\ n_H \end{pmatrix} \theta^{n_H} (1 - \theta)^{n_T}, \end{align}P(D∣θ)​=(nH​+nT​nH​​)θnH​(1−θ)nT​,​​
(2)

MLE Principle: Find θ^\hat{\theta}θ^ to maximize P(D;θ)P(D; \theta)P(D;θ), the likelihood of the data, :

θ^MLE=argmax⁡θ P(D;θ)\begin{align} \hat{\theta}_{MLE} &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \,P(D ; \theta) \end{align}θ^MLE​​=θargmax​P(D;θ)​​
(3)

Note we have P(D;θ)P(D;\theta)P(D;θ) here. ; means θ\thetaθ is a parameter of this distribution, just like μ,γ\mu, \gammaμ,γ are parameters in N(μ,γ)\mathcal{N}(\mu, \gamma)N(μ,γ). This will be different from how we view θ\thetaθ in MAP we will talk later. You can still write this as P(D∣θ) P(D\mid \theta)P(D∣θ), but just remember when we say “given θ\thetaθ” in a MLE context, we don’t mean θ\thetaθ is a Random Variable. It should be treated as a parameter.

A common procedure we take to solve a maximum problem:

  1. We don’t want to see all these products, so take the log⁡\loglog of the function to convert them into sums.
  2. Compute its derivative, and equate it with zero to find the extreme point.

Applying this procedure:

θ^MLE=argmax⁡θ P(D;θ)=argmax⁡θ(nH+nTnH)θnH(1−θ)nT=argmax⁡θ log⁡(nH+nTnH)+nH⋅log⁡(θ)+nT⋅log⁡(1−θ)combinatorial is just constant=argmax⁡θ nH⋅log⁡(θ)+nT⋅log⁡(1−θ)\begin{align} \hat{\theta}_{MLE} &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \,P(D; \theta) \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \begin{pmatrix} n_H + n_T \\ n_H \end{pmatrix} \theta^{n_H} (1 - \theta)^{n_T} \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \,\log\begin{pmatrix} n_H + n_T \\ n_H \end{pmatrix} + n_H \cdot \log(\theta) + n_T \cdot \log(1 - \theta) && \text{combinatorial is just constant}\\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \, n_H \cdot \log(\theta) + n_T \cdot \log(1 - \theta) \end{align}θ^MLE​​=θargmax​P(D;θ)=θargmax​(nH​+nT​nH​​)θnH​(1−θ)nT​=θargmax​log(nH​+nT​nH​​)+nH​⋅log(θ)+nT​⋅log(1−θ)=θargmax​nH​⋅log(θ)+nT​⋅log(1−θ)​​combinatorial is just constant​​
(4)

We can then solve for θ\thetaθ by taking the derivative and equating it with zero. This results in

nHθ=nT1−θ⟹nH−nHθ=nTθ⟹θ=nHnH+nT\begin{align} \frac{n_H}{\theta} = \frac{n_T}{1 - \theta} \Longrightarrow n_H - n_H\theta = n_T\theta \Longrightarrow \theta = \frac{n_H}{n_H + n_T} \end{align}θnH​​=1−θnT​​⟹nH​−nH​θ=nT​θ⟹θ=nH​+nT​nH​​​​
(5)

Smoothing¶

If nnn is large and your model/distribution is correct, then MLE finds the true parameters, but the MLE can overfit the data if nnn is small. It works well when nnn is large. For example, suppose you observe H,H,H,H,H. θ^MLE=nHnH+nT=55=1\hat{\theta}_{MLE} = \frac{n_H}{n_H + n_T}= \frac{5}{5} = 1θ^MLE​=nH​+nT​nH​​=55​=1.

Simple fix: We can add mmm imaginary throws and get some imaginary results based on our intuition about the distribution. For example, if we have a hunch that that θ\thetaθ is close to 0.5. Call our intuition probability θ′\theta'θ′. The mmm imaginary throws will result in mθ′m\theta'mθ′ heads and m(1−θ′)m(1-\theta')m(1−θ′) tails. Add these imaginary results to our data, so

θ^=nH+mθ′nH+nT+m\hat{\theta} = \frac{n_H + m\theta'}{n_H + n_T + m}θ^=nH​+nT​+mnH​+mθ′​
(6)

For large nnn, this is an insignificant change. For small nnn, it incorporates your “prior belief” about what θ\thetaθ should be.

This idea of “prior belief” actually gives rise to another class of thinking about distribution.

MAP and Coin Toss with Prior Knowledge¶

The Bayesian Way¶

  • Frequentists think θ\thetaθ is the probability distribution from where our data is drawn. Therefore, it should be treated as a constant, something we cannot control.
  • Bayesians consider it just another random variable, whose distribution can reflect our prior knowledge / assumption about the data distribution.

Formally, they model θ\thetaθ as a random variable, drawn from a distribution P(θ)P(\theta)P(θ). Note that θ\thetaθ is not a random variable associated with an event in a sample space. You can specify a prior belief P(θ)P(\theta)P(θ) defining what values you believe θ\thetaθ is likely to take on.

Now, we can look at P(θ∣D)=P(D∣θ)P(θ)P(D)P(\theta \mid D) = \frac{P(D\mid \theta) P(\theta)}{P(D)}P(θ∣D)=P(D)P(D∣θ)P(θ)​ , where

  • P(θ)P(\theta)P(θ) is the prior distribution over the parameter(s) θ\thetaθ - what I believe before seeing any data.
  • P(D∣θ)P(D \mid \theta)P(D∣θ) is the likelihood of the data given the parameter(s) θ\thetaθ - same as in MLE
  • P(θ∣D)P(\theta \mid D)P(θ∣D) is the posterior distribution over the parameter(s) θ\thetaθ - what I believe after observing the data.
  • P(D)P(D)P(D) is the total probability of drawing such a data considering all possible distributions. P(D)=∫θ′P(D∣θ′)P(θ′)P(D) = \int_{\theta'}P(D|\theta')P(\theta')P(D)=∫θ′​P(D∣θ′)P(θ′), but this value is just a constant in our maximization problem and we can ignore it.

A natural choice for the prior P(θP(\thetaP(θ) is the Beta distribution

P(θ)=θα−1(1−θ)β−1B(α,β)\begin{align} P(\theta) = \frac{\theta^{\alpha - 1}(1 - \theta)^{\beta - 1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)} \end{align}P(θ)=B(α,β)θα−1(1−θ)β−1​​​
(7)

where B(α,β)=Γ(α)Γ(β)Γ(α+β)B(\alpha, \beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}B(α,β)=Γ(α+β)Γ(α)Γ(β)​ is the normalization constant. Why do we choose this complicated thing as our prior belief?

For a Beta Distribution P(θ)∝θα−1(1−θ)β−1P(\theta) \propto \theta^{\alpha - 1}(1 - \theta)^{\beta - 1}P(θ)∝θα−1(1−θ)β−1. α\alphaα 和 β\betaβ 就是 θ\thetaθ 和 1−θ1-\theta1−θ 两个方向的权重, relatively

  • α>β:θ→1\alpha \gt \beta: \theta \to 1α>β:θ→1
  • β>α:θ→0\beta \gt \alpha : \theta \to 0β>α:θ→0
  • β=α:θ→0.5\beta = \alpha : \theta \to 0.5β=α:θ→0.5

假设 α=3,β=1\alpha =3, \beta=1α=3,β=1, P(θ)=∝θ2(1−θ)0=θ2P(\theta) = \propto \theta^{2}(1 - \theta)^{0} = \theta^{2}P(θ)=∝θ2(1−θ)0=θ2. 观察到 θ\thetaθ 越大 P(θ)P(\theta)P(θ) 越大，即越大的 θ \thetaθ 越容易被取到

Maximum a Posteriori Probability Estimation (MAP)¶

MAP Principle: Find θ^\hat{\theta}θ^ that maximizes the posterior distribution P(θ∣D)P(\theta \mid D)P(θ∣D):

θ^MAP=argmax⁡θ P(θ∣D)=argmax⁡θP(D∣θ)P(θ)P(D)By Bayes rule=argmax⁡θ log⁡P(D∣θ)+log⁡P(θ)Ignore Constant D, Take log\begin{align} \hat{\theta}_{MAP} &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \,P(\theta \mid D) \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \frac{P(D\mid \theta) P(\theta)}{P(D)} && \text{By Bayes rule} \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \, \log P(D \mid \theta) + \log P(\theta) && \text{Ignore Constant $D$, Take log} \end{align}θ^MAP​​=θargmax​P(θ∣D)=θargmax​P(D)P(D∣θ)P(θ)​=θargmax​logP(D∣θ)+logP(θ)​​By Bayes ruleIgnore Constant D, Take log​​
(8)

For out coin flipping scenario, we get:

θ^MAP=argmax⁡θ  P(θ∣Data)=argmax⁡θ  log⁡(P(Data∣θ))+log⁡(P(θ))=argmax⁡θ  nH⋅log⁡(θ)+nT⋅log⁡(1−θ)+(α−1)⋅log⁡(θ)+(β−1)⋅log⁡(1−θ)=argmax⁡θ  (nH+α−1)⋅log⁡(θ)+(nT+β−1)⋅log⁡(1−θ)⟹θ^MAP=nH+α−1nH+nT+β+α−2\begin{align} \hat{\theta}_{MAP} &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \;P(\theta | Data) \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \;\log(P(Data | \theta)) + \log(P(\theta)) \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \;n_H \cdot \log(\theta) + n_T \cdot \log(1 - \theta) + (\alpha - 1)\cdot \log(\theta) + (\beta - 1) \cdot \log(1 - \theta) \\ &= \operatorname*{argmax}_{\theta} \;(n_H + \alpha - 1) \cdot \log(\theta) + (n_T + \beta - 1) \cdot \log(1 - \theta) \\ &\Longrightarrow \hat{\theta}_{MAP} = \frac{n_H + \alpha - 1}{n_H + n_T + \beta + \alpha - 2} \end{align}θ^MAP​​=θargmax​P(θ∣Data)=θargmax​log(P(Data∣θ))+log(P(θ))=θargmax​nH​⋅log(θ)+nT​⋅log(1−θ)+(α−1)⋅log(θ)+(β−1)⋅log(1−θ)=θargmax​(nH​+α−1)⋅log(θ)+(nT​+β−1)⋅log(1−θ)⟹θ^MAP​=nH​+nT​+β+α−2nH​+α−1​​​
(9)

A similar calculation will give us

P(θ∣D)∝P(D∣θ)P(θ)∝θnH+α−1(1−θ)nT+β−1\begin{align} P(\theta \mid D) \propto P(D \mid \theta) P(\theta) \propto \theta^{n_H + \alpha -1} (1 - \theta)^{n_T + \beta -1} \end{align}P(θ∣D)∝P(D∣θ)P(θ)∝θnH​+α−1(1−θ)nT​+β−1​​
(10)

We notice:

  • The MAP estimate is identical to MLE with α−1\alpha-1α−1 hallucinated heads and β−1\beta-1β−1 hallucinated tails
  • As n→∞n \rightarrow \inftyn→∞, θ^MAP→θ^MLE\hat\theta_{MAP} \rightarrow \hat\theta_{MLE}θ^MAP​→θ^MLE​ as our prior knowledge α−1\alpha-1α−1 and β−1\beta-1β−1 become irrelevant compared to very large nH,nTn_H,n_TnH​,nT​.
  • MAP is a great estimator if an accurate prior belief is available (and mathematically tractable).

“True” Bayesian approach¶

Note that MAP is only one way to get an estimator in Bayesian way. There is much more information in P(θ∣D)P(\theta \mid D)P(θ∣D) and we threw away most of them by taking only the max. A true Bayesian approach is to use the posterior predictive distribution P(θ∣D)P(\theta\mid D)P(θ∣D) directly to make prediction about the label YYY of a test sample XXX based on dataset DDD. Simply put, it integrates over all possible models θ\thetaθ to make a prediction. Mathematically, we can represent it as:

P[Y∣(D,X)]=∫θP[(Y,θ)∣(D,X)]dθ(P(A)=∫BP(A,b)db)=∫θP[(Y∣θ),(D,X)]P(θ∣D)dθ(Chain rule: P(A,B∣C)=P(A∣B,C)P(B∣C))\begin{align} P[Y\mid (D,X)] = &\int_{\theta}P[(Y,\theta) \mid (D,X)] \hspace{0.05in} d\theta && (P(A) = \int_{B}P(A,b) db) \\ = &\int_{\theta} P[(Y \mid \theta), (D,X)] \hspace{0.05in} P(\theta | D) \hspace{0.05in} d\theta && \textrm{(Chain rule: $P(A,B|C)=P(A|B,C)P(B|C)$)} \end{align}P[Y∣(D,X)]==​∫θ​P[(Y,θ)∣(D,X)]dθ∫θ​P[(Y∣θ),(D,X)]P(θ∣D)dθ​​(P(A)=∫B​P(A,b)db)(Chain rule: P(A,B∣C)=P(A∣B,C)P(B∣C))​​
(11)

Intuition behind each step is:

P[Y∣(D,X)]given our data D and a test point X, estimate x’s label Y=∫θP[(Y,θ)∣(D,X)]dθhow do we estimate? we use some model θ=∫θP[Y∣(θ,D,X)]P(θ∣D)dθhow do we get θ? based on training data D\begin{align} &P[Y\mid (D,X)] && \text{given our data $D$ and a test point $X$, estimate x's label $Y$} \\ = &\int_{\theta}P[(Y,\theta) \mid (D,X)] \hspace{0.05in} d\theta && \text{how do we estimate? we use some model $\theta$} \\ = &\int_{\theta} P[Y \mid (\theta,D,X)] \hspace{0.05in} P(\theta | D) \hspace{0.05in} d\theta && \text{how do we get $\theta$? based on training data $D$} \end{align}==​P[Y∣(D,X)]∫θ​P[(Y,θ)∣(D,X)]dθ∫θ​P[Y∣(θ,D,X)]P(θ∣D)dθ​​given our data D and a test point X, estimate x’s label Yhow do we estimate? we use some model θhow do we get θ? based on training data D​​
(12)

Unfortunately, the above is generally intractable.

Summary¶

  • MLE Prediction: P[(y∣xt);θ]P[(y|x_t);\theta]P[(y∣xt​);θ] Learning: θ=argmax⁡θP(D;θ)\theta=\operatorname*{argmax}_\theta P(D;\theta)θ=argmaxθ​P(D;θ). Here θ\thetaθ is purely a model parameter.
  • MAP Prediction: P[y∣(xt,θ)]P[y|(x_t,\theta)]P[y∣(xt​,θ)] Learning: θ=argmax⁡θP(θ∣D)∝P(D∣θ)P(θ)\theta=\operatorname*{argmax}_\theta P(\theta|D)\propto P(D \mid \theta) P(\theta)θ=argmaxθ​P(θ∣D)∝P(D∣θ)P(θ). Here θ\thetaθ is a random variable.
  • “True Bayesian” Prediction: P(y∣xt,D)=∫θP(y∣θ)P(θ∣D)dθP(y|x_t,D)=\int_{\theta}P(y|\theta)P(\theta|D)d\thetaP(y∣xt​,D)=∫θ​P(y∣θ)P(θ∣D)dθ. Here θ\thetaθ is integrated out - our prediction takes all possible models into account.

As always the differences are subtle. In MLE we maximize log⁡[P(D;θ)]\log\left[P(D;\theta)\right]log[P(D;θ)] in MAP we maximize log⁡[P(D∣θ)]+log⁡[P(θ)]\log\left[P(D|\theta)\right]+\log\left[P(\theta)\right]log[P(D∣θ)]+log[P(θ)]. So essentially in MAP we only add the term log⁡[P(θ)]\log\left[P(\theta)\right]log[P(θ)] to our optimization. This term is independent of the data and penalizes if the parameters, θ\thetaθ deviate too much from what we believe is reasonable.
